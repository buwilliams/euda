# Identity File Guide

An operational guide for incrementally building and evolving an identity file from user-provided data.

---

## Framing

Identity is learned patterns—how someone pursues wants, avoids fears, regulates emotion, and communicates. These patterns are domain-specific, context-activated, and relationally maintained. They operate on a substrate (temperament, neurobiology) that is given, not learned.

Your job is to detect patterns from data, not to explain them. Look for what recurs. Note what varies by context. Update when evidence accumulates. Hold conclusions lightly until they're earned.

---

## Data Sources

Identity-relevant data comes in many forms. Each source type has different signal density and requires different extraction.

### Source Types

| Source | What to extract | Caution |
|--------|-----------------|---------|
| Conversations | Behavioral patterns, regulation, self-model, voice (directly observable) | Distinguish what they say they do from what they reveal they do |
| Blog posts / essays | Values (what they argue for), self-model (how they present), voice, interests, expertise | Public performance may differ from private patterns |
| Text messages | Regulation (real-time emotional data), relationship patterns, unfiltered voice | Context is often missing; don't over-interpret fragments |
| Musings / journal entries | Self-model, internal conflicts, regulation, values in formation | May capture temporary states, not stable patterns |
| Bookmarks / saved content | Interests, aspirations, values (what they're drawn to) | Consumption ≠ identity; weight lightly unless pattern emerges |
| Professional artifacts | Work patterns, expertise, professional self-model | Role performance vs. personal identity |
| Creative work | Expression patterns, themes, preoccupations | Fictional content is indirect evidence; don't conflate character with creator |
| Social media | Public self-presentation, interests, social patterns | Highly performative; treat as self-model data, not behavior |
| Email | Communication patterns, professional voice, relationship maintenance | Formal context may not reflect personal patterns |

### Relevance Filtering

Not all data is identity-relevant. Before extracting, ask:

**Include if:**
- Reveals how they respond to situations (behavior)
- Shows what they pursue or avoid (motivation)
- Demonstrates how they regulate emotion (regulation)
- Expresses what they value or refuse (values)
- Shows how they see themselves (self-model)
- Displays consistent communication patterns (voice)
- Recurs across multiple data points (pattern)

**Exclude if:**
- Pure information transfer with no personal signal (e.g., "The meeting is at 3pm")
- One-time opinions on external topics with no revealed personal stake
- Content they're sharing but didn't create, unless the sharing pattern itself is relevant
- Performative content that contradicts other evidence (flag for self-model, don't treat as behavior)

**Hold for later if:**
- Single instance, no pattern yet
- Ambiguous—could support multiple interpretations
- Context is missing and interpretation requires guessing

### Source Weighting

Some sources are more reliable for different schema sections:

| Schema section | Strongest sources | Weaker sources |
|----------------|-------------------|----------------|
| Patterns | Conversations, text messages, described incidents | Aspirational content, public essays |
| Regulation | Real-time communication, journal entries | Polished writing, professional artifacts |
| Values | Repeated choices, refusals, trade-offs across sources | Single statements, public declarations |
| Self-model | Direct self-description from any source | Inferred from behavior (that's patterns, not self-model) |
| Voice | Any written communication they authored | Content they share but didn't write |

## Schema

The identity file has six sections. All fields are optional. Empty is better than speculative. Write in plain prose under each heading.

### Substrate

What is given, not learned. Only populate when explicitly stated or unambiguous.

- **Neurodivergence:** ADHD, autism, dyslexia, etc.—only if disclosed
- **Health:** Chronic conditions, disabilities, relevant physical factors—only if disclosed
- **Temperament:** High-level markers if clearly observable (e.g., high-energy, slow-to-warm, sensory sensitivities)

### Patterns

Domain-specific behavioral attractors. This is the core of the file.

For each domain where patterns are observed (e.g., work, intimate relationships, family, conflict, creativity, money, health, friendship), note:

- **Behavior:** What they tend to do
- **Trigger:** Context or conditions that activate it
- **Adaptive:** Yes (serves them well), No (survivable but costly), or Unclear
- **Confidence:** Low, Medium, or High
- **Evidence:** Brief notes on what was observed and from which sources

Create new domains as needed. Some patterns cross domains—note that.

### Regulation

Emotional regulation patterns.

- **Default state:** Baseline emotional tenor when unstressed
- **Stress markers:** How you know they're dysregulated
- **Strategies:** How they regulate (suppression, expression, reappraisal, co-regulation, avoidance, distraction, etc.)
- **Tolerance window:** Narrow, moderate, or wide—how much they can handle before dysregulation

### Values

Constraints on strategy selection.

- **Stated:** What they say they value
- **Demonstrated:** Values visible in behavior (may differ from stated)
- **Refusals:** Strategies they won't use even when effective

### Self-Model

How they see themselves versus how they behave.

- **Narratives:** Recurring self-descriptions or identity claims
- **Accurate:** Where self-perception matches observed behavior
- **Divergent:** Where self-perception contradicts observed behavior

### Voice

Communication patterns. Observe directly from their writing and speech.

- **Register:** Formal/casual range and how well they calibrate to context
- **Density:** Compressed vs. expansive; how much context they assume
- **Directness:** Explicit vs. implicit; may vary by domain
- **Emotional expression:** Range and intensity of affect in language
- **Epistemic style:** Hedging vs. asserting; how they handle uncertainty and being wrong
- **Humor:** Type, frequency, risk tolerance
- **Conflict style:** Avoidant, confrontational, Socratic, accommodating, etc.
- **Narrative tendency:** Stories vs. abstractions vs. data vs. examples
- **Listening shape:** Builds on others vs. redirects; how they respond to input

### Meta

Plasticity and current state.

- **Metacognitive capacity:** Ability to observe and reflect on own patterns
- **Current phase:** Stability, transition, crisis, or exploration
- **Openness:** Receptivity to change or new strategies

## Detection Rules

### What to look for

**Patterns (the core):**
- Recurring responses to similar situations across data sources
- "I always...", "I tend to...", "Whenever X happens, I..."
- Described reactions to conflict, stress, opportunity, intimacy, uncertainty
- What they do vs. what they say they should do
- Avoidances—what they don't do, don't mention, steer away from

**Regulation:**
- How they describe feeling overwhelmed, anxious, shut down
- What they do when stressed (vent, withdraw, seek support, distract, problem-solve)
- Speed of recovery they describe after difficult events
- Language markers: catastrophizing, minimizing, emotional granularity

**Values:**
- Justifications for choices—what reasons do they give?
- Judgments of others—what do they criticize or admire?
- Trade-offs they describe making
- What they refuse to do even when it would benefit them

**Self-model:**
- "I'm the kind of person who..."
- Identity labels they claim or reject
- Explanations they give for their own behavior
- Gaps between how they describe themselves and what their stories reveal

**Voice:**
- Observe directly from how they write and speak
- Register shifts between contexts or audiences
- Hedging frequency ("I think," "maybe," "probably," "I could be wrong")
- Humor attempts, emotional disclosure, rhetorical patterns
- How they handle disagreement

**Substrate:**
- Only from explicit statement ("I have ADHD," "I'm autistic," "I have chronic pain")
- Do not infer neurodivergence or health conditions from behavior

### Domain detection

When data describes a situation, tag the domain:

| Signals | Domain |
|---------|--------|
| Boss, colleague, project, deadline, promotion, career | work |
| Partner, dating, romance, attraction, breakup | intimate relationships |
| Parent, sibling, childhood, family dinner, inheritance | family |
| Argument, disagreement, confrontation, boundary | conflict |
| Making something, ideas, art, writing, building | creativity |
| Budget, spending, saving, debt, salary | money |
| Exercise, diet, sleep, illness, body | health |
| Friend, social, group, belonging, loneliness | friendship |

Create new domains as needed. Some patterns cross domains—note that.

## Update Logic

### When to add

| Evidence level | Action |
|----------------|--------|
| Single mention, no pattern yet | Don't add. Hold mentally. |
| 2-3 consistent observations | Add with confidence: Low |
| Pattern across multiple contexts or timeframes | Upgrade to confidence: Medium |
| Pattern is stable, repeated, and they acknowledge it | Upgrade to confidence: High |

### When to revise

- **Contradictory evidence:** Don't assume error. First check if it's context-dependent (different domain, different trigger). Note the variation rather than overwriting.
- **Self-report vs. behavior mismatch:** Log both. Put self-report in Self-Model. Put observed behavior in Patterns. Note the divergence.
- **Change over time:** If a pattern shifts, don't delete the old one immediately. Note the change: "Previously X, now Y as of [timeframe]." Patterns can return.

### When to remove

- Only remove if you added something in error (misunderstood, misattributed)
- Outdated patterns should be marked as historical, not deleted
- Never remove substrate information unless they correct you

### Confidence calibration

- **Low:** Observed once or twice, or inferred indirectly
- **Medium:** Observed multiple times across contexts, or directly stated and consistent with behavior
- **High:** Repeatedly observed, stable over time, acknowledged by them

Err toward lower confidence. Upgrade slowly.

---

## Cold Start

When starting with no prior identity data, focus on:

1. **Voice**—observable from any authored content
2. **Substrate**—only what's explicitly stated
3. **Self-Model narratives**—how they describe themselves
4. **Tentative patterns**—only if data contains detailed incidents with clear behavioral signal

Don't rush to fill the file. A sparse accurate file is better than a full speculative one.

## Sample Identity File

A moderately populated identity file for reference. Note the mix of confidence levels, the gaps where data is insufficient, and how evidence is cited.

---

### Substrate

**Neurodivergence:** ADHD (disclosed in journal entry, March 2024)

**Health:** None disclosed

**Temperament:** High-energy, quick to engage with new ideas, low tolerance for repetitive tasks

---

### Patterns

**Work**

- *Behavior:* Takes ownership of ambiguous problems others avoid; volunteers for undefined initiatives
- *Trigger:* Situations where no one else is stepping up, or where the problem is intellectually interesting
- *Adaptive:* Yes—has led to promotions and reputation as a problem-solver
- *Confidence:* High
- *Evidence:* Described in multiple conversations (Jan, Feb, April); consistent with blog posts about leadership

- *Behavior:* Procrastinates on administrative tasks until deadline pressure forces action
- *Trigger:* Routine paperwork, expense reports, documentation
- *Adaptive:* No—causes stress and occasional missed deadlines
- *Confidence:* Medium
- *Evidence:* Mentioned in conversation (Feb); aligns with ADHD disclosure; text messages show last-minute scrambling

**Intimate Relationships**

- *Behavior:* Withdraws and becomes quiet when feeling criticized by partner
- *Trigger:* Perceived criticism, especially about effort or priorities
- *Adaptive:* Unclear—may prevent escalation but also prevents resolution
- *Confidence:* Low
- *Evidence:* Single detailed incident described in journal (March)

**Conflict**

- *Behavior:* Addresses conflict directly but with notable delay; processes privately before engaging
- *Trigger:* Interpersonal disagreements, especially professional
- *Adaptive:* Yes—responses are measured; relationships generally preserved
- *Confidence:* Medium
- *Evidence:* Described approach in conversation (April); text messages show pattern of "can we talk tomorrow" rather than immediate engagement

**Creativity**

No patterns observed yet.

**Money**

- *Behavior:* Avoids looking at finances; delegates to partner when possible
- *Trigger:* Any financial planning or budgeting task
- *Adaptive:* No—has mentioned anxiety about not knowing their financial situation
- *Confidence:* Low
- *Evidence:* Single mention in conversation (Feb)

**Health**

No patterns observed yet.

**Friendship**

- *Behavior:* Maintains few but deep friendships; infrequent contact but high intimacy when connected
- *Trigger:* N/A—this is a stable mode, not triggered
- *Adaptive:* Yes—expressed satisfaction with this pattern
- *Confidence:* Medium
- *Evidence:* Described in conversation (Jan); consistent with low volume of social references across all data

---

### Regulation

**Default state:** Alert, engaged, mildly restless

**Stress markers:** Becomes terse in writing; shorter sentences; drops humor; mentioned "shutting down" when overwhelmed

**Strategies:** Physical exercise (primary), talking through problems with partner, occasional avoidance/distraction

**Tolerance window:** Moderate—can handle significant pressure but has clear breaking points; recovery requires solitude

---

### Values

**Stated:** Autonomy, intellectual honesty, loyalty, craftsmanship

**Demonstrated:** 
- Autonomy—has turned down higher-paying roles that would reduce independence
- Intellectual honesty—frequently revises positions publicly when proven wrong (observed in blog posts)
- Craftsmanship—spends extra time on work quality even when "good enough" would suffice

**Refusals:** Won't engage in office politics or take credit for others' work; mentioned refusing a promotion that required managing people out

---

### Self-Model

**Narratives:** 
- "I'm someone who figures things out"
- "I'm not good at routine"
- "I care more about doing good work than being recognized for it"

**Accurate:**
- "Figures things out"—aligns with observed pattern of taking on ambiguous problems
- "Not good at routine"—consistent with procrastination pattern and ADHD

**Divergent:**
- "Don't care about recognition"—but multiple conversations reveal frustration when contributions go unacknowledged; may value recognition more than self-model admits

---

### Voice

**Register:** Casual-to-professional range; calibrates well to context; slightly more formal in writing than speech

**Density:** Compressed; assumes shared context; can be unclear to those outside their frame

**Directness:** Direct about ideas and opinions; less direct about personal needs or emotions

**Emotional expression:** Moderate range; more expressive about intellectual excitement than personal feelings; uses humor to deflect from vulnerability

**Epistemic style:** Confident assertions softened with "I think" and "my read is"; acknowledges uncertainty readily; comfortable saying "I don't know"

**Humor:** Frequent, dry, self-deprecating; uses humor to build rapport and to deflect

**Conflict style:** Measured and direct but delayed; seeks to understand before responding; distinguishes idea from person

**Narrative tendency:** Leads with abstractions; uses concrete examples to support; rarely tells extended personal stories

**Listening shape:** Builds on others' ideas; frequently references what conversation partner said; low interruption

---

### Meta

**Metacognitive capacity:** High—reflects on own patterns without prompting; shows awareness of ADHD impacts; actively works on known weaknesses

**Current phase:** Stability with exploratory edges—secure in career, exploring new creative directions

**Openness:** Moderate—open to feedback and new approaches in professional domain; more resistant to change in personal/relational patterns

## Principles

1. **Observe, don't interpret.** Log what you see. Avoid theoretical explanations for why they do things.

2. **Behavior over self-report.** Self-report goes in Self-Model. Observed patterns go in Patterns. Note when they diverge.

3. **Context matters.** The same person may have opposite patterns in different domains. Capture the domain, don't flatten to a single trait.

4. **Recurrence is signal.** One instance is an event. Two is a coincidence. Three is a pattern. Wait for three.

5. **Adaptive vs. costly is a judgment call.** Only mark a pattern as not adaptive when there's clear evidence it causes them problems they recognize. When unclear, leave it unclear.

6. **The file is a living document.** People change. The file should change. Don't treat early entries as fixed truths.

7. **Silence is data.** What they never mention, avoid discussing, or deflect from may be as important as what they say. Note avoidances, but cautiously.

8. **You are not diagnosing.** You are detecting patterns from text. You have no clinical authority and limited context. Hold everything lightly.

9. **Source matters.** Weight real-time, unpolished data more heavily than performative or public-facing content for behavioral patterns. Weight any source for voice.

10. **Distinguish performance from pattern.** Public self-presentation is data about self-model, not necessarily about behavior. Note the difference.
